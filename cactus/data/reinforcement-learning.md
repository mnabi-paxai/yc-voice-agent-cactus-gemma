# Reinforcement Learning

## Overview
Reinforcement Learning (RL) is a paradigm of machine learning where an "agent" learns to make decisions by performing actions in an "environment" to achieve a goal. Unlike other forms of machine learning that rely on labeled data, RL agents learn through trial and error. They receive "rewards" for desirable actions and "penalties" (or negative rewards) for undesirable ones. The agent's primary objective is to maximize the cumulative reward it receives over time, discovering the best sequence of actions, or "policy," through repeated interactions with its environment. This learning process is often compared to how humans or animals learn from experience, adapting their behavior based on consequences.

## Key Ideas
- **Learning through Trial and Error:** The concept emphasizes learning by trying different actions and observing their outcomes, rather than being explicitly told what to do. This is exemplified by the observation of octopuses.
- **Absence of Explicit Reward Signals:** In many real-world scenarios, and as noted with octopuses, learners do not receive direct, pre-defined reward signals from an external "teacher" (like parents). Instead, they infer what constitutes a reward or penalty from their interactions with the environment.
- **Analogy to Animal Learning:** The session highlighted a strong parallel between the way octopuses learn independently through exploration and the core principles of reinforcement learning.
